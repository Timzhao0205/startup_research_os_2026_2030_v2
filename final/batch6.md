## Key Findings
- **The U.S. FDA AI-enabled device market is overwhelmingly radiology and 510(k)-driven.** Per The Imaging Wire (March 11, 2026), the FDA's end-December 2025 update lists exactly **1,451 AI-enabled medical devices** authorized "since it began keeping track in 1995," of which **1,104 (76%) are radiology devices**; 2025 alone saw a record 295 new clearances (75% radiology), with GE HealthCare leading at 120 radiology AI authorizations, Siemens Healthineers at 89, and Philips at 50. The peer-reviewed 2024 cohort (Biomedicines 2025, BM-039) confirms 510(k) dominance: among 168 ML-enabled devices cleared in 2024, the median submission-to-clearance time was 162 days, with 510(k) at a median 151 days versus 372 days for De Novo (p<0.001). *Inference: a genuine regulatory/hardware wedge outside radiology imaging is comparatively rare and therefore strategically valuable.*
- **Expedited pathways are real but narrow.** Per the peer-reviewed review PMC12119601 (BM-030), "from 2015 to 2024, only 12.3% of the 1,041 BDP-designated devices received marketing authorization (n=128 as of September 2024), with mean decision times of 152, 262, and 230 days for 510(k), de novo, and PMA pathways respectively — significantly faster than standard approvals for de novo (338 days) and PMA (399 days)." The De Novo pathway classifies ~26 requests/year vs ~2,929 510(k) clearances/year (~0.89% the size, BM-029). China's NMPA green channel cut review time materially and, per Navigator Global/Cisema, "in 2025…the NMPA, through its CMDE, released 12 batches of announcements admitting 104 products into the Special Review Procedure for Innovative Medical Devices," with updated Implementation Rules issued April 2025 (BM-046).
- **The EU MDR/IVDR transition is a structural commercialization bottleneck** (staggered deadlines through 2027–2029; EUDAMED modules becoming mandatory ~May 2026), while **reimbursement is the true adoption gate.** Per "Who Will Pay for AI?" (Radiology: AI 2021, PMC8166111, BM-037), CMS finalized the first AI-specific CPT code on Dec 1, 2020 for IDx-DR (now LumineticsCore), code **92229**, and established the first AI New Technology Add-on Payment for **Viz.ai's Viz LVO** large-vessel-occlusion stroke algorithm (Sept 2020).
- **Hospital/lab operational pain is well-evidenced** and distinct from TAM hype. Per Alshammari et al. (Journal of Healthcare Leadership 2022, PMC9109973, BM-034), a two-site study found that "with total automation, the number of tests performed per single worker has increased to an average of 1.4 and 3.7 times…in the clinical chemistry and serology sections, respectively (p ≤ 0.001)," against a documented backdrop where clinical-lab retirements outpace graduates (ADLM whitepaper, BM-045).

### Regulatory and standards spine (Tier 1)
The FDA's AI-enabled device list (BM-001) is the authoritative catalog and is explicitly "not a comprehensive resource," updated periodically. The FDA finalized its **Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP)** guidance for AI-enabled device software in December 2024 (BM-003), grounded in FDORA §515C, allowing pre-authorized model updates without new submissions; a broader draft PCCP guidance for all medical devices followed in August 2024 (BM-004). The **Good Machine Learning Practice** 10 guiding principles were finalized by IMDRF (N88) in January 2025 (BM-015, BM-007/008), originating from a 2021 FDA/Health Canada/MHRA collaboration. IMDRF's SaMD framework (BM-012/013/016) defines four risk categories (I–IV) and the clinical-evaluation model adopted globally; N81 (2025) broadened scope to "medical device software" generally (BM-014).

The **EU MDR/IVDR** transition (BM-017) staggers deadlines (higher-risk MDR devices to Dec 31, 2027; IVDR class D to 2027, C to 2028, B/A-sterile to 2029) with EUDAMED modules becoming mandatory around May 2026 — a documented capacity-and-timing bottleneck (fact). **WHO IVD prequalification** (BM-018/019) gates UN procurement for a defined disease scope (HIV, HCV, HBsAg, malaria, HPV, G6PD, cholera, syphilis, HBV NAT), with performance evaluation separating from the PQ procedure in 2026.

**China NMPA**: the "green channel"/Special Review Procedure for Innovative Medical Devices has operated since 2014, offering a faster review and a dedicated reviewer; updated Implementation Rules were issued April 2025 and 104 innovative devices were admitted across 12 batches in 2025 (BM-046, fact). A regulatory-data analysis (BM-041, preprint) maps China's medical-device software/AI approvals trajectory via the "21"-prefix MDSW classification; MERICS (BM-042) documents centralized procurement (volume-based purchasing) and localization as deliberate industrial-policy levers favoring domestic players, noting ~85% of medical care is provided by public hospitals.

### FDA market structure and expedited pathways
The FDA list reached **1,451 cumulative AI/ML devices through end-2025**, radiology ~76% (1,104 devices), ~96% via 510(k) (BM-048 triangulating BM-001; BM-039 peer-reviewed 2024 cohort). The **Breakthrough Devices Program** (BM-009/010, BM-030, BM-044) granted 1,041 designations (2015–2024) but only 12.3% (128 devices) reached market. A Stanford Law analysis (BM-044) further flags that roughly one in ten authorized breakthrough devices have already been recalled and that many enter via 510(k) without clinical trials. A 27-year recall analysis (BM-038) corroborates post-market governance gaps for AI/ML devices.

### Bio-instrumentation, sensors, robotics, diagnostics (Tier 1 primary literature)
Wearable electrochemical biosensors now demonstrate continuous multi-analyte monitoring (BM-020, Caltech "NutriTrek" for sweat amino acids/vitamins; BM-022 microneedle interstitial-fluid arrays; BM-023 a 2026 wireless, battery-free regenerable sweat sensor monitoring urea/lactate/glucose/cortisol up to 21 days; BM-021 review). Surgical robotics evaluation frameworks (BM-024 IDEAL Robotics Colloquium in Nature Medicine; BM-025 Science Robotics on AI/RL/IL autonomy; BM-026 R-TAMIS systematic review) show the field maturing toward anatomy-specific and semi-autonomous systems with unresolved clinical-evidence and human-factors challenges. Point-of-care/microfluidic diagnostics reviews (BM-027/028) and NIH NIBIB's POCTRN/RADx infrastructure (BM-031/032/033) anchor the translational pipeline. Head-to-head CGM evidence (BM-040) supports a hardware+data wedge in chronic-disease monitoring. IEEE TBME (BM-049) defines the bioinstrumentation/biosensor scholarly scope.

### Operational pain and reimbursement
Total lab automation raised tests-per-worker 1.4x (chemistry) and 3.7x (serology) (BM-034) amid documented staffing shortages where retirements outpace graduates (BM-045). On reimbursement, CMS established CPT 92229 for autonomous diabetic-retinopathy screening and the Viz LVO NTAP, and in 2021 waived the "substantial clinical improvement" criterion for Breakthrough-designated devices (BM-035/036/037) — making coding/coverage, not clearance, the binding adoption constraint.
